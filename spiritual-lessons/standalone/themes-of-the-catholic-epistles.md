## What Does "Catholic" Mean?

The word **Catholic** (Greek: *katholikos*) means **universal** — pertaining to the whole. In the context of the New Testament, the Catholic Epistles are the letters written not to a specific church (like Paul's letters to Corinth or Ephesus) but to the **universal Church** — to all believers everywhere.

The seven Catholic Epistles are:
1. **James**
2. **1 Peter**
3. **2 Peter**
4. **1 John**
5. **2 John**
6. **3 John**
7. **Jude**

These letters were written by the Pillars of the Jerusalem Church — James (the brother of the Lord), Peter (the chief Apostle), John (the Beloved Disciple), and Jude (the brother of James and of the Lord).

## The Authors

| Letter | Author | Audience |
|--------|--------|---------|
| James | James, brother of the Lord, first bishop of Jerusalem | "The twelve tribes in the Dispersion" — Jewish Christians scattered |
| 1 Peter | Simon Peter, Apostle | "Elect exiles of the Dispersion" — Christians in Asia Minor |
| 2 Peter | Simon Peter, Apostle | The universal Church; a farewell letter near his death |
| 1 John | John the Apostle and Evangelist | The universal Church; combating early Gnostic ideas |
| 2 John | John the Apostle | "The elect lady and her children" — possibly a specific church |
| 3 John | John the Apostle | Gaius — a specific church leader |
| Jude | Jude, brother of James (and of the Lord) | The universal Church; combating false teachers |

## Five Major Themes

### Theme 1: Trials and Suffering

> "Count it all joy, my brothers, when you meet trials of various kinds, for you know that the testing of your faith produces steadfastness."
> — James 1:2–3

> "In this you rejoice, though now for a little while, if necessary, you have been grieved by various trials, so that the tested genuineness of your faith — more precious than gold that perishes though it is tested by fire — may be found to result in praise and glory and honor at the revelation of Jesus Christ."
> — 1 Peter 1:6–7

The Catholic Epistles were written to communities under pressure — dispersed, persecuted, socially marginalized. The consistent message: **suffering is not meaningless**. It tests, refines, and produces what cannot be produced any other way.

**Coptic liturgical connection:** The Fast of Kiahk (the fast before Christmas) includes readings from these epistles, connecting the Church's seasonal fasting and the themes of patient endurance.

### Theme 2: Faith and Works

> "So also faith by itself, if it does not have works, is dead."
> — James 2:17

> "And besides this, giving all diligence, add to your faith virtue, to virtue knowledge, to knowledge self-control, to self-control perseverance, to perseverance godliness, to godliness brotherly kindness, and to brotherly kindness love."
> — 2 Peter 1:5–7

These letters consistently resist a "faith alone" that divorces belief from action. Genuine faith produces a transformed life. The man who says he has faith but has no works is like a person who says they are alive but does not breathe.

### Theme 3: Love and Unity

> "Little children, let us not love in word or talk but in deed and in truth."
> — 1 John 3:18

> "Above all, keep loving one another earnestly, since love covers a multitude of sins."
> — 1 Peter 4:8

Love is not sentiment — it is action. And it is communal: the love that the Catholic Epistles call for is directed toward the **brothers and sisters** in the community of faith. The first test of love for God is love for the person sitting next to you.

### Theme 4: False Teachings

> "For certain people have crept in unnoticed who long ago were designated for this condemnation, ungodly people, who pervert the grace of our God into sensuality and deny our only Master and Lord, Jesus Christ."
> — Jude 1:4

> "But false prophets also arose among the people, just as there will be false teachers among you, who will secretly bring in destructive heresies, even denying the Master who bought them."
> — 2 Peter 2:1

The Catholic Epistles are intensely concerned with **protecting the deposit of faith** — the truth once for all delivered to the saints (Jude 1:3). False teachers were a constant threat in the early Church, and the authors are unsparing in their warnings.

**Connection to today:** The willingness to clearly name false teaching is not unkindness — it is pastoral love for the flock.

### Theme 5: Worldliness

> "Do not love the world or the things in the world. If anyone loves the world, the love of the Father is not in him. For all that is in the world — the desires of the flesh and the desires of the eyes and pride of life — is not from the Father but is from the world."
> — 1 John 2:15–16

> "You adulterous people! Do you not know that friendship with the world is enmity with God?"
> — James 4:4

The world (*kosmos*) in the New Testament refers not to the physical creation (which God loves — John 3:16) but to **the system of values and desires that is organized apart from God**. The Catholic Epistles consistently draw a sharp line between the Kingdom of God and the kingdom of this world.

## Matching to the Coptic Liturgical Calendar

The Coptic Church reads from the Catholic Epistles throughout the liturgical year. Key connections:

| Season | Themes | Relevant Epistles |
|--------|--------|------------------|
| Great Lent | Repentance, trials, self-control | James, 1 Peter |
| Pascha | New life, resurrection hope, love | 1 John |
| Pentecost | The Spirit, unity, gifts | 2 Peter |
| Apostles' Fast | Persecution, witness, holding the faith | 1 Peter, Jude |
| Kiahk | Patient waiting, hope, endurance | 1 Peter, 2 Peter |

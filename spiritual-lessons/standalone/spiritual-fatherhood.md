## The Text (1 Corinthians 4:14–16)

> "I do not write these things to make you ashamed, but to admonish you as my beloved children. For though you have countless guides in Christ, you do not have many fathers. For I became your father in Christ Jesus through the gospel. I urge you, then, be imitators of me."
> — 1 Corinthians 4:14–16

---

## Father vs. Instructor (Paidagogos)

Paul distinguishes between two relationships:

- **Countless guides** — the Greek is **παιδαγωγοί** (*paidagōgoi*), from which we get "pedagogue." In the ancient world, the *paidagōgos* was a slave who accompanied the child to school, enforced discipline, and ensured the child did not wander. He was not the teacher — he was the escort and enforcer.

- **Not many fathers** — the father is something else entirely. He begets, he pours himself into, he shapes not through external constraint but through relationship and love.

| Paidagōgos (Instructor/Guide) | Father |
|-------------------------------|--------|
| External enforcement | Internal formation |
| Temporary (until maturity) | Permanent relationship |
| Concerned with behavior | Concerned with the person |
| Can be replaced | Irreplaceable |
| Gives rules | Gives himself |

Paul says: you have had many instructors. But you have one father. And that father-child relationship is the model for spiritual formation.

## The Chain of Fatherhood

Spiritual fatherhood is not an invention — it is a transmission. It flows in a chain:

> "And what you have heard from me in the presence of many witnesses entrust to faithful men, who will be able to teach others also."
> — 2 Timothy 2:2

**The chain:** God the Father → Christ → the Apostles → the Church Fathers → the priests/bishops → the faithful

Paul was Timothy's spiritual father:

> "To Timothy, my true child in the faith..."
> — 1 Timothy 1:2

Timothy was to be a father to others. The chain continues to us.

Every priest is called to be a spiritual father in this sense — not merely an administrator of sacraments, but a father who begets spiritual children through the Gospel, forms them through relationship, and pours his life into theirs.

## Confession and Spiritual Fatherhood

The sacrament of confession is the most intimate expression of spiritual fatherhood. In confession:

- The priest does not merely pronounce absolution (though he does that)
- He listens, discerns, counsels, and guides
- He takes responsibility for the soul before him
- He prays for this soul beyond the confessional

The ideal confession is not a "transaction" — sin listed, absolution pronounced. It is a conversation between a child and a father, in which the father helps the child see more clearly, love God more deeply, and live more faithfully.

This requires both parties:
- **The penitent** who comes with honesty and openness
- **The priest-father** who comes with patience, wisdom, and prayer

## Priesthood and Fatherhood

The title "**Abouna**" (Coptic/Arabic for "our father") given to every Coptic priest is not merely honorific — it is a theological claim. The priest is called to be a father.

This does not compete with the universal fatherhood of God (Matthew 23:9 — "call no man father"). St. Paul himself uses the title in 1 Cor 4:15, referring to his spiritual relationship with the Corinthians. The prohibition in Matthew 23 is against claiming the kind of ultimate, originating fatherhood that belongs to God alone — not against all uses of the relational term.

The priest as father:
- Generates new life in baptism (John 3:5 — born of water and Spirit)
- Nourishes through the Eucharist and the Word
- Restores in confession
- Sends out to the world through the dismissal

## We as Fathers in Service

Spiritual fatherhood is not only the priest's calling. Anyone who:
- Teaches a child in Sunday school
- Mentors a younger believer
- Leads a small group or youth meeting
- Disciples a new Christian

...is exercising a form of spiritual fatherhood. The distinction is not ordination — it is relationship. You can be a spiritual father or mother to someone without a clerical title.

Paul says: **"Be imitators of me"** (1 Cor 4:16). This is the most demanding statement of fatherhood. Not "follow my advice." Not "do what I say." But: watch my life, see how I live, and pattern yourself after that.

The spiritual father's most powerful tool is not his words — it is his life.

---

## The Characteristics of a Spiritual Father/Mother

Drawing from the Fathers and the desert tradition:

1. **Knows you** — a father who does not know his child cannot guide him. The relationship requires time, honesty, and ongoing conversation.
2. **Prays for you** — the father bears the child in prayer, not only in conversation.
3. **Is honest with you** — a father who tells you only what you want to hear is not a father; he is a flatterer.
4. **Points you to Christ** — the goal of the father's guidance is never dependence on the father but growth toward Christ.
5. **Is themselves under a father** — no one is too mature for a spiritual father. The elder who has no elder has himself as a fool for a guide.

## Reflection Questions

1. Do you have a spiritual father — a priest or elder with whom you have a real, ongoing relationship of guidance and prayer?
2. Are you a spiritual father/mother to anyone? Who are you investing in?
3. What is the difference between receiving religious information and being formed by a father?
4. Paul says "be imitators of me." Could you say this to someone — "imitate my life"? If not, what needs to change first?

## Preamble: On Losing Hope

Thirty-eight years. That is how long the man had been lying at the Pool of Bethesda. The Mishnah teaches that forty years is a generation. Thirty-eight years is almost a generation of waiting, of watching others be healed, of losing hope in increments too small to measure.

Before we come to the encounter itself, we must sit with this reality: some of us are carrying something for a very long time. And Jesus goes to Bethesda specifically.

---

## The Pool of Bethesda (John 5:1–4)

> "Now there is in Jerusalem by the Sheep Gate a pool, in Aramaic called Bethesda, which has five roofed colonnades. In these lay a multitude of invalids — blind, lame, and paralyzed."
> — John 5:2–3

Bethesda means "House of Mercy" or "House of Grace." The five colonnades were filled with suffering people, all waiting for the movement of the water.

Archaeological excavations in Jerusalem have confirmed the existence of this pool — one of the first-century sites that matches John's Gospel description precisely.

## Four Encounter Points

### 1. Christ Went to Him (v. 6)

> "When Jesus saw him lying there and knew that he had already been there a long time..."
> — John 5:6

Jesus **knew**. He did not stumble across this man. He saw him, knew his history, and chose him.

This is the first lesson: Christ initiates. He came to Bethesda. He came to the colonnade where the most broken were lying. He does not wait for the paralyzed man to reach Him — the man cannot.

The initiative is always God's. "We love because he first loved us" (1 John 4:19).

### 2. "Do You Want to Be Made Well?" (v. 6)

> "Jesus said to him, 'Do you want to be made well?'"
> — John 5:6

This seems like the most unnecessary question in the Gospels. He has been lying here for thirty-eight years.

But it is not unnecessary. Jesus asks it because:

- **He honors human choice.** He does not heal anyone who does not want to be healed. The will matters.
- **He forces the man to articulate his desire.** "What do you want?" is a clarifying question.
- **He prepares the man for something he has stopped believing possible.** After 38 years, had he truly stopped hoping?

The question is still asked of us: *Do you want to be made well?*

Wanting to be well is more complicated than it sounds:
- We can be comfortable in our brokenness
- Our struggles can become our identity
- We may not believe healing is actually available to us

### 3. "Rise, Take Up Your Bed and Walk" (v. 8)

> "Jesus said to him, 'Rise, take up your bed, and walk.'"
> — John 5:8

The man's answer to "do you want to be made well?" was technically an excuse: "I have no one to help me into the pool" (v. 7). He explained why it wasn't working. He blamed others. He gave reasons.

Jesus does not engage the excuse. He gives a command that is impossible:

- **"Rise"** — the man cannot rise; he is paralyzed
- **"Take up your bed"** — he has been lying on it for 38 years
- **"Walk"** — he has not walked in decades

The command creates the capacity. Obedience precedes understanding.

> "And at once the man was healed, and he took up his bed and walked."
> — John 5:9

The healing happens the moment he begins to obey — not after he understands how, not after he builds strength, not after he is comfortable.

### 4. "Sin No More" (v. 14)

> "Afterward Jesus found him in the temple and said to him, 'See, you are well! Sin no more, that nothing worse may happen to you.'"
> — John 5:14

The post-miracle encounter happens in the **temple**. The man, newly healed, went to the temple. Jesus finds him there.

The warning: "Sin no more, that nothing worse may happen to you." This is not saying his paralysis was caused by sin (contrast with John 9:3 — the man born blind). It is saying: you have been given physical healing; protect your spiritual health with even greater care.

The greater paralysis is not of the body.

## The Critics: The Sabbath Controversy (vv. 10–16)

The Pharisees confront the healed man: "It is the Sabbath, and it is not lawful for you to take up your bed" (v. 10). They are focused on the mat, not the miracle.

He tells them: "The man who healed me said to me, 'Take up your bed and walk'" (v. 11). They ask who it was. He did not know — Jesus had withdrawn into the crowd.

Later, when the man finds out and tells the authorities it was Jesus, "the Jews were persecuting Jesus, because he was doing these things on the Sabbath" (v. 16).

The miracle that should have been cause for worship became cause for prosecution.

## Spiritual Application

1. **Are you at Bethesda?** — Waiting, discouraged, blaming others, watching others be healed while you wait?
2. **Do you want to be made well?** — Honestly. Is there a comfort in staying where you are?
3. **Rise** — Is there an area of your life where Christ is giving you a command that requires a step of faith before you feel ready?
4. **Sin no more** — Have you received a healing (physical, relational, spiritual) and returned to the patterns that led to brokenness?

> "The one who had been healed did not know who it was."
> — John 5:13

Even before he knew Jesus' name, he was healed. Our understanding of Christ deepens after the healing begins, not before. Take the step.

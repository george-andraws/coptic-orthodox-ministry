## The Text (Luke 24:13–35)

> "That very day two of them were going to a village named Emmaus, about seven miles from Jerusalem, and they were talking with each other about all these things that had happened. While they were talking and discussing together, Jesus himself drew near and went with them. But their eyes were kept from recognizing him."
> — Luke 24:13–16

> "And beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he interpreted to them in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself."
> — Luke 24:27

> "When he was at table with them, he took the bread and blessed and broke it and gave it to them. And their eyes were opened, and they recognized him. And he vanished from their sight."
> — Luke 24:30–31

> "They said to each other, 'Did not our hearts burn within us while he talked to us on the road, while he opened to us the Scriptures?'"
> — Luke 24:32

---

## Context: The Day of Resurrection

This encounter happens on the same day as the Resurrection — Easter Sunday. The two disciples (one named Cleopas, one unnamed — perhaps the Evangelist himself?) are walking **away** from Jerusalem. They are leaving.

They had hoped: "We had hoped that he was the one to redeem Israel" (v. 21). Their hope is in the past tense. They are finished.

---

## Act 1: Christ Approaches (vv. 15–16)

> "Jesus himself drew near and went with them."
> — Luke 24:15

The verb "drew near" (*ἐγγίσας, engisas*) is important. Christ does not wait for them to turn around and come back to Jerusalem. He goes after them. He walks beside them in their grief and confusion.

Their eyes are "kept from recognizing him" — this is a divine passive; God keeps them from recognizing Jesus until the appointed moment. The recognition must come in the right way.

**The road is the place of encounter.** Christ meets us not only in the Temple, the church, or the structured time of prayer — but on the road, in the middle of our ordinary movement through the world.

---

## Act 2: Scripture as the Road (vv. 17–27)

Christ walks with them and asks what they are discussing. They are astonished: "Are you the only visitor to Jerusalem who does not know the things that have happened?" (v. 18).

Christ asks: "What things?"

They tell Him everything — the arrest, the crucifixion, the empty tomb, the women's report. They have all the facts. They are missing the interpretation.

> "And beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he interpreted to them in all the Scriptures the things concerning himself."
> — Luke 24:27

Christ opens the Scriptures — the Old Testament, which they already knew — and shows them that the Cross was not the failure of their hopes, but the fulfillment of God's plan.

### The Function of Scripture in This Story

- Scripture is the road: understanding the Word of God is how we travel toward recognition of Christ
- Without Scripture, the facts are devastating: "He died." **With** Scripture, they are glorious: "He had to die, and rise."
- The Scriptures do not merely *describe* Christ — they *open* and *reveal* Him to us

> "Did not our hearts burn within us while he talked to us on the road, while he opened to us the Scriptures?"
> — Luke 24:32

The burning heart is the response to Scripture rightly received — not merely read for information, but opened by Christ Himself.

---

## Act 3: The Eucharist as Meeting Christ (vv. 28–31)

They arrive at the village. Christ acts as though He will go further. They constrain Him: "Stay with us, for it is toward evening and the day is now far spent" (v. 29).

He stays. They sit at table. And then:

> "He took the bread and blessed and broke it and gave it to them. And their eyes were opened, and they recognized him. And he vanished from their sight."
> — Luke 24:30–31

The verbs "took, blessed, broke, gave" are the exact verbs of the Last Supper and the feeding of the five thousand. This is deliberate. The recognition happens at the **breaking of bread** — the Eucharist.

### The Eucharist as the Place of Recognition

We cannot manufacture the burning heart on demand. But the Church gives us the structure:
1. **The Liturgy of the Word** — Christ opens the Scriptures to us
2. **The Liturgy of the Eucharist** — Christ is known in the breaking of bread

Every Divine Liturgy is the road to Emmaus re-enacted. Christ walks with us through the Scriptures, then reveals Himself in the bread and cup.

---

## Act 4: Return to Jerusalem (vv. 33–35)

They cannot stay at Emmaus — they return to Jerusalem immediately. The encounter with Christ is never for private consumption; it always sends us back to the community.

> "And they rose that same hour and returned to Jerusalem."
> — Luke 24:33

They went back to report what had happened — and found that Christ had appeared to Peter as well.

---

## Mother's Day Connection

This lesson was given near Mother's Day. The connection: the women at the tomb were the first to know and the first to report. Their testimony was the first news of the Resurrection. The mothers in our community — who bring children to Liturgy, who pray through the night, who keep the faith in the home — are doing the same work: carrying the news that "He is risen."

---

## A Brief Prayer to the Theotokos

O Most Holy Theotokos, you who carried the Word made flesh — intercede for us, that our hearts may burn as we walk the road, and that our eyes may be opened to recognize your Son in the breaking of bread. Amen.

## Reflection Questions

1. Are you walking **toward** Jerusalem or **away** from it right now? Where are you going?
2. What does it mean that Christ "opened" the Scriptures? Do you experience the Scriptures as opened or closed?
3. When was the last time your heart "burned within you" — in prayer, in Scripture, in the Eucharist?
4. After Emmaus, they returned to Jerusalem immediately. What does the encounter with Christ send you back to?

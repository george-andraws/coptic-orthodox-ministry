## The Central Question of the Gospels

> "He said to them, 'But who do you say that I am?'"
> — Matthew 16:15

Of all the questions Jesus asked, this is the most important — the one on which everything else depends. It is the question every person must answer, and no answer is neutral.

---

## Context: Caesarea Philippi (Matthew 16:13–17)

Caesarea Philippi was a deliberately chosen location. It was:
- A pagan city, built by Philip the Tetrarch in honor of Caesar
- Near the source of the Jordan River
- A site of worship of the god Pan — with a cave called "the Gates of Hades"

Against this backdrop — the gates of paganism and death — Jesus asks His question and receives Peter's confession. The placement is a theological statement: **the confession of Christ is made against every alternative, in the shadow of every rival claim**.

---

## Two Questions (Matthew 16:13–15)

### Question 1: "Who do people say the Son of Man is?"

> "And they said, 'Some say John the Baptist, others say Elijah, and others Jeremiah or one of the prophets.'"
> — Matthew 16:14

The crowd's answers are not hostile — they are complimentary. John the Baptist, Elijah, Jeremiah — these are honored figures. But they are all **wrong**. Being highly regarded is not the same as being correctly identified.

Many people today hold high opinions of Jesus while stopping short of the full confession:
- "A great moral teacher"
- "A revolutionary social reformer"
- "One of the great prophets"
- "A wise sage"

These are not correct answers — because they cannot account for the Resurrection and the claims of the Gospels.

### Question 2: "But who do **you** say that I am?"

The shift from "people" to "you" is the most important word in the question. The first question is about public opinion. The second is personal. You cannot shelter behind what others believe. The question comes to each individual.

**"But"** — the great adversative. It creates a contrast. Whatever the crowds say, whatever the experts say, whatever your family says — **who do you say that I am?**

---

## Peter's Confession

> "Simon Peter replied, 'You are the Christ, the Son of the living God.'"
> — Matthew 16:16

Two titles, each carrying enormous weight:

**"The Christ"** (*Christos*) — the Greek translation of the Hebrew *Mashiach* (Messiah). The Anointed One — the king promised to David (2 Samuel 7), the suffering servant of Isaiah 53, the Son of Man of Daniel 7. The fulfillment of every prophetic hope of Israel.

**"The Son of the living God"** — not merely a human messiah, but the divine Son. The "living God" is a title that emphasizes the contrast with the dead idols of paganism — and "Son" is Peter's confession of Christ's unique divine sonship.

Jesus' response:

> "Blessed are you, Simon Bar-Jonah! For flesh and blood has not revealed this to you, but my Father who is in heaven."
> — Matthew 16:17

Peter did not work this out by intellectual effort. It was revealed to him. Faith in Christ is always a gift of grace — we cannot reason our way to the full confession; we must receive it.

---

## The Transformation Point in Mark

In Mark's Gospel (which many scholars consider the first Gospel written and the basis for Matthew), the confession at Caesarea Philippi (Mark 8:27–30) is the **literary turning point** of the entire Gospel. Before it, Jesus is asking "Who am I?" by His miracles and teaching. After it, He turns His face toward Jerusalem and begins to speak of His suffering, death, and resurrection. Peter's confession is the hinge on which the entire story turns.

---

## "I Preach Christ and Him Crucified" (1 Corinthians 1:23–24)

> "But we preach Christ crucified, a stumbling block to Jews and folly to Gentiles, but to those who are called, both Jews and Greeks, Christ the power of God and the wisdom of God."
> — 1 Corinthians 1:23–24

Paul's summary of the Gospel: **Christ, crucified**. Not Christ the teacher. Not Christ the example. Not Christ the social reformer. Christ crucified — the God who died and rose.

This is the stumbling block and the foolishness. It is also the only answer to the question "Who do you say that I am?" that the rest of the New Testament can sustain.

---

## St. John Chrysostom's Commentary

> "Consider this: Jesus asked the disciples about the opinions of others first — not to learn what they thought, for He knew — but to distance the disciples from the error of the crowd before asking them directly. He did not want them to be influenced by others' answers when they gave their own."
> — St. John Chrysostom

This is why He asks in two stages. He prepares the disciples to give an independent answer, not an echo of popular opinion.

## Wrong Answers — Ancient and Modern

| Who Jesus Is Not | Who Claims This |
|------------------|-----------------|
| "A good moral teacher" — nothing more | Many secular people today |
| "An Essene revolutionary" | Some historical critics |
| "One prophet among many" | Islam, liberal theology |
| "A cosmic Christ without a historical body" | Gnostics, New Age movements |
| "A social reformer" | Liberation theology (in some forms) |
| "A great example but not God" | Jehovah's Witnesses, Arians |

All of these honor Jesus in some way. None of them answer His question correctly. And the answer matters eternally.

## The Question Comes to You

> "But who do you say that I am?"

Not: what do you know about Him? Not: what did you learn in Sunday school? Not: what does your family believe?

**Who do YOU say that I am?**

This is the question of a lifetime — not a one-time confession. It must be re-answered in every circumstance:
- When you suffer and it seems God is absent: *who do you say He is?*
- When the culture mocks your faith: *who do you say He is?*
- When prayer seems unanswered: *who do you say He is?*
- At the moment of death: *who do you say He is?*

The answer is the same — and it is everything:

> "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God."

## Reflection Questions

1. Have you personally answered this question — not as a memory verse, but as a living confession?
2. In your daily life, do you live as if Jesus is the Christ, the Son of the living God — or as if He is something less?
3. What wrong answer are you most tempted to settle for? "A good teacher"? "An example"? "God of Sunday but not Monday"?
4. The Father revealed this to Peter. Have you asked the Father to reveal Christ to you?

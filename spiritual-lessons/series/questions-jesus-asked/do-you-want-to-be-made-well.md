## The Situation: Pool of Bethesda (John 5:1–15)

> "Now there is in Jerusalem by the Sheep Gate a pool, in Aramaic called Bethesda, which has five roofed colonnades. In these lay a multitude of invalids — blind, lame, and paralyzed."
> — John 5:2–3

The pool of Bethesda was believed to have healing properties when its water was stirred — "whoever stepped in first after the stirring of the water was healed" (v. 4). Five colonnades filled with suffering people, all waiting for the movement of the water.

Among them: a man who had been ill for **thirty-eight years**.

---

## The Question (v. 6)

> "When Jesus saw him lying there and knew that he had already been there a long time, he said to him, 'Do you want to be made well?'"
> — John 5:6

This question seems unnecessary — even cruel. Of course he wants to be healed. He has been here thirty-eight years.

But Jesus never asks unnecessary questions. He is not asking about the obvious surface desire. He is asking something deeper:

- **Do you want the disruption that healing brings?** A healed man must work, must take responsibility, must live differently.
- **Have you given up?** After thirty-eight years, has the illness become his identity?
- **Do you truly believe it is possible for you?** Not in theory — but for you, today?

The question honors the man's **will**. Jesus does not heal against anyone's will. He waits for the yes.

---

## The Answer: Blaming Others (v. 7)

> "The sick man answered him, 'Sir, I have no one to put me into the pool when the water is stirred up, and while I am going another steps down before me.'"
> — John 5:7

He does not say "yes." He explains why he cannot be healed. He names who is at fault: no one to help him. Others who get there first. Circumstance. System. Others.

This is not a malicious answer — it is honest. But it is also a deflection. He answers the question of *desire* with an analysis of *obstacles*.

How often do we do this?

- "I want to pray more, but I'm so busy"
- "I want to give more, but my finances are tight"
- "I want to fix this relationship, but they never reach out first"

The obstacles are real. But they are not what Jesus asked about. He asked about the *will*.

---

## The Healing: Outside the Comfort Zone (vv. 8–9)

> "Jesus said to him, 'Rise, take up your bed, and walk.'"
> — John 5:8

Jesus does not engage the excuses. He gives a command:

1. **Rise** — from the ground, from the position of the paralyzed, from the posture of waiting
2. **Take up your bed** — the bed that defined his illness now becomes something he carries; the symbol of his helplessness becomes something he controls
3. **Walk** — movement, direction, action

The command was impossible. The man had not walked in thirty-eight years. But:

> "And at once the man was healed, and he took up his bed and walked."
> — John 5:9

The healing happens **at once** — the moment he began to obey. The capacity came with the command. He did not need to understand the mechanism; he needed to begin to move.

### Outside the Comfort Zone

The man's healing required him to do things he had never done in nearly four decades:
- Stand up
- Bend down and pick up his mat
- Walk through a crowd

All of this was, physiologically, impossible. The healing required a step beyond what he knew himself to be capable of.

This is consistently true in the Gospels: the leper was healed "as he went" (Luke 17:14), not before. The blind man's sight came after he washed in the pool. The healing is often in the going, not before the going.

---

## Post-Miracle: The Temple Visit (vv. 10–15)

The Pharisees confronted the healed man for carrying his mat on the Sabbath. He replied: "The man who healed me said to me, 'Take up your bed and walk'" (v. 11). They asked who it was. He did not know.

Later, Jesus found him in the temple — the man went to the temple after his healing. And Jesus gave him a final word:

> "See, you are well! Sin no more, that nothing worse may happen to you."
> — John 5:14

**"Sin no more"** — the healing of the body is not the final goal. The healing of the soul is. The question "do you want to be made well?" was always about more than the paralysis.

---

## Reflection

The question comes to each of us:

**"Do you want to be made well?"**

Not: do you want to feel better? Not: do you want your circumstances to improve? But: **do you want to be made whole** — even if wholeness requires you to rise, take up your mat, and walk in ways you have never walked before?

1. What is your "thirty-eight years"? What have you been waiting for God to change for a long time?
2. Is your answer to Jesus a desire — or a list of obstacles?
3. What is the command Jesus is giving you right now, that you have been afraid to obey?
4. After the healing — the man went to the temple. After God acts in your life, where do you go?

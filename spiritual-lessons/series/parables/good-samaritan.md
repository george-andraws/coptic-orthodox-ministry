## The Text (Luke 10:25–37)

> "And behold, a lawyer stood up to put him to the test, saying, 'Teacher, what shall I do to inherit eternal life?'"
> — Luke 10:25

Two questions frame the parable:
1. "What shall I do to inherit eternal life?" (v. 25)
2. "And who is my neighbor?" (v. 29)

The second question — "who is my neighbor?" — is asked in self-justification. The lawyer wanted to limit the scope of love. Jesus refuses to answer the question as asked. He ends with a different question: **"Which of these three do you think was a neighbor to the man who fell into the hands of robbers?"**

The parable shifts from *who deserves my love* to *who am I called to become*.

## The Allegorical Reading: Christ as the Samaritan

The Church Fathers universally read this parable as an allegory of salvation. The Samaritan is Christ.

> "The man going down from Jerusalem to Jericho is Adam. Jerusalem is Paradise, from which he fell. Jericho is the world. The robbers are the enemy and his demons... The priest is the Law, the Levite the Prophets, the Samaritan Christ himself."
> — Origen of Alexandria

| Character / Element | Allegorical Meaning |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Man going down from Jerusalem | Adam / humanity, going down from paradise |
| Jerusalem | Paradise, the original state of grace |
| Jericho | The fallen world |
| The robbers | The devil and his angels |
| Stripped, beaten, half-dead | Humanity stripped of virtue, wounded by sin |
| The priest | The Law — which could point but not heal |
| The Levite | The Prophets — which could announce but not save |
| The Samaritan | Christ — the unexpected one, despised and rejected |
| The wine | The Blood — poured out for cleansing |
| The oil | The mercy of God / the Holy Spirit |
| The inn | The Church |
| The innkeeper | The apostles / bishops / priests |
| Two denarii | The two commandments of love, or the two Testaments |
| "I will return" | The Second Coming |

> "The Samaritan who cared for the man who had been wounded represents Christ, who came not from a Samaritan village but from heaven."
> — St. Ambrose of Milan

## The Seven Verbs of Mercy (v. 33–35)

When the Samaritan saw the wounded man, seven verbs describe his response:

1. **He saw him** — attentive vision; he did not look away
2. **He had compassion** (ἐσπλαγχνίσθη, *esplanchnisthē*) — lit. "his bowels were moved" — the deepest compassion, felt physically
3. **He went to him** — he crossed to the other side
4. **He bound up his wounds** — direct care, not delegation
5. **He poured on oil and wine** — healing with what he had
6. **He set him on his own animal** — he gave his own transport, he walked
7. **He took care of him** — at the inn, all night

> "Notice that the Samaritan gave the man a place on his own animal. He did not send for a stretcher. He gave up his own comfort."
> — St. Augustine

## Oil and Wine as Sacraments

> "He poured on oil and wine."
> — Luke 10:34

The Fathers consistently read these as sacramental:

- **Wine** = the Blood of Christ, poured out for the remission of sins. It cleanses and disinfects.
- **Oil** = the grace of the Holy Spirit, poured in anointing (Baptism, Chrismation, the Unction of the Sick). It soothes and heals.

The Church continues this work: "I will repay you" — the Samaritan (Christ) continues to care for us through the Church's sacramental life.

---

## The Inn as the Church

> "He brought him to an inn and took care of him."
> — Luke 10:34

> "The inn that accepts all who come is the Church, because in the Church all are welcomed who are burdened by sins."
> — St. Jerome

The inn:
- Receives the wounded without examination of worthiness
- Is maintained by the innkeeper (the bishop / priest)
- Is sustained by the Samaritan's ongoing provision
- Is a place of healing, not of judgment

---

## "Who Is My Neighbor?" — The Reversed Question

At the end, Jesus does not answer "who is my neighbor?" He asks: **"Which of the three was a neighbor to the man?"**

The lawyer said: "The one who showed mercy."

Jesus said: **"Go and do likewise."**

The question was never "who deserves my love?" The question is: **"To whom can I go?"**

## Patristic Voices

> "We ourselves are the man who went down from Jerusalem and fell among thieves. But Christ, passing by, found us half-dead and did not pass us by."
> — St. Cyril of Alexandria

> "The commandment is not merely 'feel compassion.' It is 'go and do.' The movement is essential."
> — St. John Chrysostom

---

## Spiritual Application

1. **You are the man half-dead on the road** — before you are the Good Samaritan, receive the mercy of Christ first.
2. **The Church is the inn** — do you use the sacraments (oil and wine) for your healing?
3. **Who have you passed by?** The priest and Levite passed by for religious reasons. What are yours?
4. **Go and do likewise** — not "feel and intend likewise." The parable ends with action.

**This week:** Identify one person in your life who is "half-dead on the road." Cross to their side.

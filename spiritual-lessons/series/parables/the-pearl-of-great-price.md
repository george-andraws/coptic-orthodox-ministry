# The Hidden Treasure and the Pearl of Great Price: Two Parables, Not Just One

## Two Parables Together (Matthew 13:44–46)

> "The kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field, which a man found and covered up. Then in his joy he goes and sells all that he has and buys that field."
> — Matthew 13:44

> "Again, the kingdom of heaven is like a merchant in search of fine pearls, who, on finding one pearl of great value, went and sold all that he had and bought it."
> — Matthew 13:45–46

These two parables are always given together — and they belong together. They are not simply the same point repeated. They describe two fundamentally different ways of finding the Kingdom.

---

## The Hidden Treasure: Unexpected Discovery

The man in the first parable was **not looking for treasure**. He was working in a field — digging, plowing, doing ordinary work. He stumbled upon treasure he was not seeking.

This is the experience of many who come to faith:
- **St. Peter and Andrew** — they were fishing. Jesus walked by. "Follow me."
- **Matthew the tax collector** — he was sitting at the tax booth, doing his ordinary work. "Follow me."
- **Zacchaeus** — he climbed a tree out of curiosity. Jesus stopped beneath it and called him by name.

They were not searching for the Kingdom of God. The Kingdom found them.

---

## The Pearl: Lifelong Seeking

The merchant in the second parable was **actively searching for fine pearls**. This was his business, his vocation, his life's pursuit. He had seen many pearls. He knew quality when he saw it.

When he found this one pearl — surpassing all the others — he immediately knew: this is worth everything.

This is the experience of those who came to faith through searching:
- **St. Augustine** — years of philosophy, Manichaeism, and intellectual seeking before his conversion in the garden. When he finally found Christ, he wrote: "Our heart is restless until it rests in Thee."
- **Malcolm Muggeridge** — a journalist and intellectual who spent decades skeptical of Christianity before his conversion.

The merchant had been preparing for this discovery his whole life. When it came, he was ready to act on it.

---

## What Unites the Two: Selling Everything with Joy

Despite the different paths — stumbling upon it vs. searching for it — both men respond identically:

1. They recognize the surpassing value of what they have found
2. They **sell everything** to obtain it
3. They do so **with joy** (the first parable makes this explicit: "in his joy he goes and sells all that he has")

This is the theological heart of the two parables. The surrender is total — and it is **joyful**, not reluctant.

> "Indeed, I count everything as loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord. For his sake I have suffered the loss of all things and count them as rubbish."
> — Philippians 4:8

St. Paul uses remarkably strong language: *skybala* — "rubbish," even "dung." Everything he had counted as gain he now considers worthless in comparison to Christ. And he considers this a **gain**, not a loss.

---

## Recognizing the Value

The key question both parables implicitly ask: **do you recognize what you have?**

> "Where your treasure is, there your heart will be also."
> — Matthew 6:21

If you treat the Christian life as an add-on — something to manage alongside your real priorities — you have not yet recognized the value of what you have found. You are the person who found treasure and left it in the field.

The pearl merchant knew a pearl of surpassing value when he saw it. Do we?

Signs that we may not be recognizing the value:
- The Eucharist feels routine rather than astonishing
- Scripture is a duty rather than a delight
- Church attendance is a social obligation rather than an encounter with the living God
- Prayer is something we fit in rather than what we build our day around

---

## "He Sells All" — The Cost

Both parables insist: **you cannot keep everything and have the Kingdom**. There is a cost.

But the cost is described in the context of joy — not sacrifice, not loss, not reluctant duty. The man **sells** what he had; he is not robbed. He makes an active, free, joyful choice because he has recognized the relative value of what he is giving up and what he is gaining.

> "Whoever finds his life will lose it, and whoever loses his life for my sake will find it."
> — Matthew 10:39

The surrender is real. The joy is also real. Both are true simultaneously.

---

## Reflection Questions

1. How did you "find" the Kingdom — through unexpected discovery, or through seeking? What does that story say about God?
2. Is there something you are holding back from the transaction — something you will not "sell" in order to fully have the Kingdom?
3. Do you experience your faith as the "pearl of great price" — worth more than everything — or as one good thing among many?
4. The field-finder sold everything "in his joy." Is your relationship with God characterized by joy? If not, what is missing?

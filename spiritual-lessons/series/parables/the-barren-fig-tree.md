## Context: Repentance in Luke 13

The parable of the barren fig tree is introduced in the context of two events Jesus mentions — a massacre by Pilate and a tower that fell:

> "Do you think that these Galileans were worse sinners than all the other Galileans, because they suffered in this way? No, I tell you; but unless you repent, you will all likewise perish."
> — Luke 13:2–3

Jesus refuses the equation of suffering with sin (as He also does in John 9). But He uses these tragedies as an urgent call: **repent**. The same call comes twice (vv. 3, 5): "unless you repent, you will all likewise perish."

The parable that follows is an expansion of this urgency.

---

## The Parable (Luke 13:6–9)

> "And he told this parable: 'A man had a fig tree planted in his vineyard, and he came seeking fruit on it and found none. And he said to the vinedresser, "Look, for three years I have come seeking fruit on this fig tree, and I find none. Cut it down. Why should it use up the ground?" And he answered him, "Sir, let it alone this year also, until I dig around it and put on manure. Then if it should bear fruit next year, well and good; but if not, you can cut it down."'"
> — Luke 13:6–9

---

## The Figures in the Parable

| Character | Interpretation |
|-----------|---------------|
| **The owner of the vineyard** | God the Father |
| **The fig tree** | Israel (then) / the baptized believer (now) |
| **The vinedresser** | Christ — the intercessor who pleads for time |
| **The vineyard** | The Church; the place of privilege |
| **Three years** | The period of Christ's ministry to Israel; or the full measure of time given |
| **Fruit** | Repentance, righteousness, works of love |

---

## The Fig Tree in Its Vineyard

A fig tree planted in a **vineyard** is in the most privileged possible location. It has:
- Rich soil
- Tending by the vinedresser
- Watering and cultivation
- Protection

Yet it bears no fruit. The problem is not the environment — it is the tree.

This parallels Israel in the first century — given the Law, the Prophets, the Temple, the covenants, the very presence of the Messiah — and yet fruitless. And it parallels the baptized Christian who has received every sacramental gift and yet remains unchanged, unproductive, bearing no fruit of repentance or virtue.

---

## "Why Should It Use Up the Ground?" (v. 7)

This phrase is striking: the barren fig tree is not merely useless — it is **consuming resources** that could go to something fruitful. It takes up space in the vineyard, absorbs nutrients from the soil, occupies the vinedresser's attention.

Application: a Christian who bears no fruit is not a neutral presence in the Church. The gifts and sacraments poured into them are absorbed without return. This is not a comfortable teaching — but it is an honest one.

---

## The Intercession of the Vinedresser

The dramatic center of the parable is the vinedresser's intercession:

> "Sir, let it alone this year also."
> — Luke 13:8

The owner is ready to cut it down. The vinedresser intercedes: give it one more year. I will dig around it. I will fertilize it. Let me try once more.

This is Christ's intercession for us before the Father. **He pleads for more time.** He is willing to do the work — to dig around us, to apply what is needed — if we will receive it.

> "He always lives to make intercession for them."
> — Hebrews 7:25

The fourth year — "if not, you can cut it down" — reveals that the intercession is not unlimited. The grace given is sufficient. There is a limit to the extension of time.

---

## Parallels in Israel and the Church

### Israel Then

Jesus told this parable while His own ministry was underway — the "third year" of divine patience toward Israel. He wept over Jerusalem: "How often would I have gathered your children together as a hen gathers her brood under her wings, and you were not willing!" (Matt 23:37).

The intercession was offered. The judgment eventually came — in 70 AD, with the destruction of the Temple.

### Believers Now

The Church is the new vineyard. Baptism is the planting. Every Liturgy, every Lent, every confession is the vinedresser digging around us, applying fertilizer (the grace of the Holy Spirit).

The question is not whether God is patient — He is. The question is whether we will bear fruit during the time of intercession.

## Bearing Fruit as Christians

What does it mean to "bear fruit"?

> "Blessed is the man who walks not in the counsel of the wicked, nor stands in the way of sinners, nor sits in the seat of scoffers; but his delight is in the law of the LORD, and on his law he meditates day and night. He is like a tree planted by streams of water that yields its fruit in its season."
> — Psalm 1:1–3

> "I am the vine; you are the branches. Whoever abides in me and I in him, he it is that bears much fruit, for apart from me you can do nothing."
> — John 15:5

The fruit is:
- **Repentance** — the first fruit Jesus called for in Luke 13:3
- **Love** — "the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace..." (Galatians 5:22–23)
- **Good works** — "we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works" (Ephesians 2:10)
- **Making disciples** — "go and bear fruit — fruit that will last" (John 15:16)

## Reflection Questions

1. In what ways is God the vinedresser digging around you right now — applying pressure, creating discomfort, working on your roots?
2. What fruit are you bearing? Be honest. What is the evidence of repentance and transformation in your life?
3. The parable ends without telling us whether the fourth year brings fruit. That ending is unresolved — intentionally so. It is addressed to *you*. What will your fourth year produce?
4. Who are you interceding for — praying that God would give them more time, digging around them with your prayers and presence?

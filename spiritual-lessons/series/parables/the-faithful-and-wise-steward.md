## Context in Luke 12

Luke 12 is a chapter on undivided attention — alertness to God:
- The warning about the Pharisees' hypocrisy (vv. 1–3)
- Fear God, not men (vv. 4–12)
- The parable of the rich fool (vv. 13–21)
- Do not be anxious (vv. 22–34)
- Be ready for the master's return (vv. 35–40)
- **The parable of the faithful and wise steward (vv. 41–48)**

The chapter builds toward a single conclusion: **you do not know the hour**, so live every moment as if the master is watching — because He is.

---

## The Trigger: Peter's Question (v. 41)

> "Peter said, 'Lord, are you telling this parable for us or for all?'"
> — Luke 12:41

Jesus does not answer directly. Instead He asks:

> "Who then is the faithful and wise manager, whom his master will set over his household, to give them their portion of food at the proper time?"
> — Luke 12:42

The answer: you are. The parable is addressed to **Peter** — and to all who exercise leadership, stewardship, and responsibility in God's household.

---

## The Faithful Steward (vv. 42–44)

> "Blessed is that servant whom his master will find so doing when he comes."
> — Luke 12:43

The faithful steward is:
- **Faithful** — he does not change his behavior when the master is absent
- **Wise** — he uses good judgment in how he serves the household
- **Consistent** — "so doing when he comes" implies he has *always* been doing this, not scrambling at the last moment

His reward: the master will "set him over all his possessions" — expanded stewardship, greater trust.

---

## Three Areas of Stewardship

### 1. The Church

Those who lead, teach, and serve in the Church are stewards of God's household. The test: are you serving the household faithfully when the master is not visibly watching? Or only when there is an audience?

> "Preach the word; be ready in season and out of season."
> — 2 Timothy 4:2

### 2. The Workplace

The believer at work is also a steward — of resources, of people, of the employer's trust, of the time and attention they are paid for.

> "Whatever you do, work heartily, as for the Lord and not for men."
> — Colossians 3:23

### 3. The Family

Parents are stewards of their children — souls entrusted to them by God. The home is the primary school of faith. The faithfulness expected of a steward applies especially here.

> "And these words that I command you today shall be on your heart. You shall teach them diligently to your children."
> — Deuteronomy 6:6–7

## The Unfaithful Steward (vv. 45–46)

> "But if that servant says to himself, 'My master is delayed in coming,' and begins to beat the male and female servants, and to eat and drink and get drunk, the master of that servant will come on a day when he does not expect him and at an hour he does not know, and will cut him in pieces and put him with the unfaithful."
> — Luke 12:45–46

The unfaithful steward makes two errors:

1. **"My master is delayed"** — he concludes that the absence of accountability means the end of accountability. He is wrong.
2. **He uses his authority to abuse** — the power given for service is used for exploitation.

The one who has authority in the household — in the church, at work, in the family — will answer for how that authority was used.

---

## The Judgment Based on Knowledge (vv. 47–48)

> "And that servant who knew his master's will but did not get ready or act according to his will, will receive a severe beating. But the one who did not know, and did what deserved a beating, will receive a light beating. Everyone to whom much was given, of him much will be required, and from him to whom they entrusted much, they will demand the more."
> — Luke 12:47–48

This is one of the most important principles in the Gospels: **judgment is proportional to knowledge**.

| Level of Knowledge | Level of Responsibility | Consequence for Failure |
|--------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Knew the master's will | Highest | Severe judgment |
| Did not know | Lower | Lighter judgment |

> "To whom much is given, much is required."
> — Luke 12:48 (paraphrase)

**St. Augustine** on this passage:

> "The more light God has given you — through Scripture, through the sacraments, through a holy upbringing, through the teaching of the Church — the more He will require of you at the judgment. This is not unjust; it is proportional. Grace is not given for our comfort alone, but for our accountability."

## Practical Questions

1. **Are you a faithful steward in the Church?** Do you serve consistently, or only when there is visible accountability?
2. **Are you a faithful steward at work?** If your employer saw exactly how you spent every hour today, would they be satisfied?
3. **Are you a faithful steward in the family?** Are the souls entrusted to you being nourished — spiritually, emotionally, relationally?
4. **What have you been given?** — list your gifts, your opportunities, your knowledge of the faith. Now ask: what is being required of you with these?

> "It is required of stewards that they be found faithful."
> — 1 Corinthians 4:2

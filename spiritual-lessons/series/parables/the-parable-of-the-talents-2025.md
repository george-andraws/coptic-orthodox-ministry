## Introduction

This is a revisitation of the Parable of the Talents (Matthew 25:14–30) with a specific focus on the **one-talent servant** — who he is, why he buried his talent, what he represents for us today, and what the final judgment reveals about his error.

*(See also: [The Parable of the Talents — 2024 lesson](the-talents-2024.md) for the foundational overview)*

---

## The Text (Matthew 25:14–30)

> "He also who had received the one talent came forward, saying, 'Master, I knew you to be a hard man, reaping where you did not sow, and gathering where you scattered no seed, so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent in the ground.'"
> — Matthew 25:24–25

---

## Who Is the One-Talent Servant?

This is the character the parable lingers on — the one who receives the least and does the worst. Why?

Because he is the most dangerous character — not because he is obviously wicked, but because he *appears* righteous.

He:
- Did not steal the talent
- Did not gamble it away
- Did not spend it on himself
- Returned it intact

He is the religious person who keeps what they have received safe, unbothered, and unused.

---

## The Four Reasons He Buried the Talent

### 1. Attitude — He Misjudged the Master

> "I knew you to be a hard man."
> — Matthew 25:24

His fundamental error was **theological**: he had a wrong image of God. He saw his master as demanding, severe, and impossible to please. So he opted out of the relationship entirely — too afraid to try, convinced he would fail.

This mirrors the person who believes: "God expects too much. I can never be good enough. Better not to try."

A wrong image of God produces paralysis, not service.

### 2. Fear

> "I was afraid."
> — Matthew 25:25

Fear was his explicit reason. Not laziness (though the master calls him "slothful") — fear. Fear of failure, fear of loss, fear of the master's judgment if he tried and fell short.

But notice: the master judges him for not trying *more* harshly than he would have judged him for trying and failing. Fear of judgment led him to the very judgment he feared.

> "There is no fear in love, but perfect love casts out fear. For fear has to do with punishment, and whoever fears has not been perfected in love."
> — 1 John 4:18

### 3. Laziness

The master calls him "wicked and **slothful**" (v. 26). Laziness — *acedia* in the patristic tradition — is not mere physical inactivity. It is the refusal to engage, to risk, to invest oneself in something that matters.

Acedia says: "What's the point? Nothing I do will make a difference." It is spiritual apathy dressed as humility.

### 4. Selfishness and the Chain Reaction

The one-talent servant's choice did not only affect him. His buried talent was given to the ten-talent servant. His failure created a deficit in the community. His non-participation left something undone that was meant to be done.

Every buried talent leaves a gap in the body of Christ.

> "For the body does not consist of one member but of many... If the foot should say, 'Because I am not a hand, I do not belong to the body,' that would not make it any less a part of the body."
> — 1 Corinthians 12:14–15

## Are We the Third Servant?

The confronting question: **Are you the one-talent servant?**

Signs that you might be:
- You believe your contribution is too small to matter
- You are waiting until you are "ready enough" before you begin
- You compare your gifts to others and conclude yours are insufficient
- You have been given something (time, money, skill, influence) and are keeping it safe instead of investing it
- You fear that trying and failing would be worse than not trying at all

---

## Faith and Works: Not Opposites

The Parable of the Talents is not a parable about earning salvation by works. But it is a parable about the relationship between **genuine faith** and **visible action**.

The two faithful servants did not invest out of fear of punishment — they invested because they knew and trusted their master. Their investment was the expression of their relationship with him.

> "Faith by itself, if it does not have works, is dead."
> — James 2:17

A faith that does not produce action is not yet full faith. Genuine trust in a good master produces risk-taking, not paralysis.

### The Widow's Mite (St. John Chrysostom)

> "The widow who gave two copper coins gave more than all the others — not because two coins are more than bags of gold, but because she gave out of her poverty. What matters is not the quantity of the gift but the quality of the giver."
> — St. John Chrysostom

The one-talent servant received one talent and gave back one. The widow gave two coins — and gave back everything. The issue is not amount. It is the heart of the giver.

## The Final Judgment of the One-Talent Servant

> "You wicked and slothful servant!... So take the talent from him and give it to him who has the ten talents."
> — Matthew 25:26, 28

And then the most severe line:

> "And cast the worthless servant into the outer darkness. In that place there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth."
> — Matthew 25:30

The one-talent servant is not a villain of great evil. He is a person of **great indifference**. And in the final accounting, the indifferent person is cast out with the wicked.

This is the parable's most uncomfortable teaching: **neutrality is not safe**. Not investing is not the same as not losing. Choosing not to risk is itself a choice — with consequences.

## Practical Application

1. **Name your talent** — What has God specifically entrusted to you? Be concrete.
2. **Name your fear** — Why have you not invested it? What are you afraid of?
3. **Correct your image of God** — Is He a hard man who reaps where He does not sow? Or is He the master who entrusted you with real wealth and expects a relationship of trust and risk?
4. **Begin this week** — What is one step of investment you can take with one of your buried talents?

> "Well done, good and faithful servant."
> — Matthew 25:21

This is the sentence we are working toward. Not "well done, perfect servant." **Faithful** servant. The one who tried.

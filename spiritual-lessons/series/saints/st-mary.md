## Introduction

St. Mary is not a peripheral figure in the Coptic tradition — she is the most venerated of the saints, the Theotokos (God-bearer), and the greatest model of the Christian life. To understand her is to understand the Faith itself.

But she is also frequently misunderstood — both over-exalted (as if she were divine) and under-honored (as if she were merely a vessel). This lesson seeks the biblical and patristic middle ground.

---

## Her Virtues

### 1. Humility

Mary calls herself "the handmaid of the Lord" (Luke 1:38) — not a queen, not an empress of heaven. The first virtue of the most exalted human being is self-lowering before God.

> "For he has looked on the humble estate of his servant."
> — Luke 1:48

Her humility is not self-deprecation — it is clear-eyed truth. She knows who God is and therefore knows her proper place before Him.

### 2. Submission / Obedience

Her *fiat* — "Let it be to me according to your word" (Luke 1:38) — is the supreme act of submission in human history. It is the reversal of Eve's self-assertion: where Eve said "I will take," Mary says "Let it be done to me."

> "Behold, I am the servant of the Lord; let it be to me according to your word."
> — Luke 1:38

Her obedience was not passive compliance — it was active, costly trust. She knew what her yes would cost. She said yes anyway.

## The Annunciation (Luke 1:26–38)

The angel Gabriel appeared to Mary in Nazareth. His greeting — "Rejoice, O favored one, the Lord is with you!" — disturbed her. She "pondered what sort of greeting this might be" (v. 29).

She asked one question: "How will this be, since I am a virgin?" (v. 34). This is the question of a person who trusts God but seeks to understand His way. The angel answered: the Holy Spirit will overshadow her.

Her response: the *fiat*. No further questions. Belief and obedience.

---

## The Visitation (Luke 1:39–56)

Immediately after the Annunciation, Mary "arose and went with haste" to visit her cousin Elizabeth in the hill country of Judea. She was pregnant. She traveled a long distance — possibly 80–100 miles — to serve another.

The Magnificat (Luke 1:46–55) — her prayer upon arriving — is one of the greatest hymns of Scripture. It is the prayer of a woman who has received everything and gives all glory to God:

> "My soul magnifies the Lord, and my spirit rejoices in God my Savior."
> — Luke 1:46–47

---

## The Magi (Matthew 2:1–12)

The wise men from the East came seeking "the king of the Jews." They found the child "with Mary his mother" (Matt 2:11). She is present but in the background — as always, the one who points to her Son, not herself.

---

## The Wedding at Cana (John 2:1–11)

The first miracle of Jesus — at the instigation of His mother:

> "When the wine ran out, the mother of Jesus said to him, 'They have no wine.'"
> — John 2:3

Jesus' response: "My hour has not yet come" (v. 4). Yet He acted. Mary's intercession brought forward the hour of His first sign.

Her instruction to the servants — which is her instruction to all of us:

> "Do whatever he tells you."
> — John 2:5

This is the fullest expression of St. Mary's role: she does not draw attention to herself. She points to her Son and says: **do whatever He tells you**. She is the supreme catechist.

## Theotokos: The Theology

**Theotokos** (Greek: Θεοτόκος) — literally "God-bearer" or "Mother of God." This title was formally defined at the **Council of Ephesus (431 AD)**, one of the most important ecumenical councils.

### The Nestorian Controversy

Nestorius, Bishop of Constantinople, objected to the title "Theotokos," preferring "Christotokos" (Christ-bearer). His argument: Mary gave birth to the human nature of Christ, not to the divine nature.

### St. Cyril of Alexandria's Response

St. Cyril argued (and the Council agreed) that the two natures of Christ — divine and human — are united in one Person (hypostasis). Therefore, Mary bore a person, not merely a nature. And that person is God the Son.

To say Mary is not the Mother of God is to imply that the human Christ is a different person from the divine Christ — which is Nestorianism.

The Council decreed: **Mary is rightly called Theotokos** — God-bearer. Not because she is the source of the divine nature, but because she bore the one Person who is God the Son.

> "Theotokos is not a title of Mary so much as it is a title of Christ. It confesses that the one born of her is fully and truly God."
> — St. Cyril of Alexandria

## Intercession: Theology and Practice

### The Biblical Basis

> "The prayer of a righteous person is powerful and effective."
> — James 5:16

If the prayers of the righteous are powerful and effective, then the prayers of the most righteous human being who ever lived — the one who bore God in her womb — are powerful indeed.

> "He is not God of the dead, but of the living."
> — Matthew 22:32

The saints are not dead — they are alive in Christ. Their prayers continue. Their intercession before the throne of God is a participation in Christ's own intercession (Hebrews 7:25).

### The Coptic Practice

The Coptic Church prays to St. Mary at every Liturgy — in the *Theotokia* (hymns to the Theotokos) and in the *Lobsh* (devotional prayers). This is not worship of Mary; it is asking a friend of God to pray for us.

### "Do Whatever He Tells You" — The Role of Intercession

Mary's intercession at Cana did not override Christ's will — it aligned with it. She brought a need to her Son. He responded according to His wisdom and timing. This is all intercession is: bringing our needs to God through the prayers of those who are close to Him.

## Fasting Exercise

The Coptic Church maintains fasting in honor of the Theotokos — particularly the **Fast of the Apostles** (which includes her feast) and the **Fast of the Virgin** (the fast leading up to her Assumption/Dormition on August 22).

Consider: during this lesson, commit to one day of fasting in honor of St. Mary — in gratitude for her yes, which made our salvation possible.
